9. WHO WILL WATCH / AUDIENCE
=====================================================

Honest frame first: Seb has said he does not know who will watch. That is the correct starting point. Jazz docs do not have a mass "product-market fit" you can spreadsheet into existence. What follows are working hypotheses, not claims. They are meant to stop us pitching the film as if it were a streamer blockbuster, and to match content products to people who might actually care.

We will not know until material is out and something either lands or does not. Update this section after the first Top 30 episodes, after the trip, and after first public screenings.

-----------------------------------------------------
9.1 Primary audiences (people most likely to watch)
-----------------------------------------------------

Hypothesis, ranked by likelihood of attention, not by size of market:

A. Jazz people who already care about Chet Baker
   Collectors, players, serious listeners, forum/Reddit/Discogs types, people who own late SteepleChase and Timeless records and argue about which decade was "real." They will watch a feature if the thesis is sharp and the access is real. They will also watch Top 30 Albums episodes as standalone content. This is the core. Small, vocal, long memory. They police accuracy and they share what they trust.

B. Broader jazz documentary watchers
   People who liked Ronnie's, Blue Note: Beyond the Notes, Ken Burns Jazz clips, or music docs on Sky Arts / ARTE. They do not need to be Chet specialists. They need a clear story, faces on camera, and a reason the film exists now (last living witnesses). Slightly larger than A, still niche.

C. Robin's existing world
   Gig audiences (Cambridge/London and wherever he tours), repmusic YouTube subscribers, people who already follow his Chet songbook work, friends of the band, local jazz scenes, students and regulars. This is not "marketing." It is the only warm audience that already trusts the messenger. Path B (screenings with live set after the film) lives or dies here. Do not treat this as secondary prestige. For money and momentum it may be the strongest practical audience we have.

-----------------------------------------------------
9.2 Secondary audiences (real, thinner, different hooks)
-----------------------------------------------------

D. Music history / album culture
   People who watch long-form YouTube about records, sessions, labels, and "forgotten eras." Top 30 is the product for them. The feature is optional.

E. Human-interest / addiction-and-art story seekers
   Chet's life draws people who are not jazz fans: beauty-and-ruin, exile, last years. Let's Get Lost already owns much of that mythology. Chasing Chet should not lead with junkie tragedy for this group. If they come, they come via the European counter-argument and the living witnesses. Soft secondary. Easy to over-court and wrong-tone the film if we chase them.

F. European culture / place audiences
   Dutch, Belgian, Italian, French viewers who care that this story happened in their cities. Festival programmers and public broadcasters (ARTE, NPO, RAI, Sky Arts) respond to this. For consumers it is smaller than jazz fandom, but for Path A (festival/broadcast) it is a pitch line, not a demographic slide.

G. People adjacent to addiction research / recovery culture
   Sally Marlow's presence and the clinical framing of Baker's later life may attract a thin professional slice. Not a primary marketing target. Useful if a clip or talk lands in that world; do not build the site or ads around it.

-----------------------------------------------------
9.3 Gatekeepers are a different "audience"
-----------------------------------------------------

Festival programmers, sales agents, and broadcasters are not viewers in the consumer sense. They watch for: premiere status, thesis clarity, production competence, rights cleanliness, and whether the film can be sold as European cultural product.

What they need from us (trailer, one-pager, stills, access story, music rights path) is different from what a YouTube jazz fan needs (one good anecdote, album recommendation, face of a living sideman).

Do not design the public website only for gatekeepers, and do not design festival materials only for YouTube fans. Separate artefacts:

- Public site / Top 30 / shorts: for audiences A–D and Robin's network
- Festival one-pager, screener, trailer cut: for gatekeepers
- Press kit later: for both, after there is something to press

-----------------------------------------------------
9.4 Realistic scale (passion-funded indie jazz doc)
-----------------------------------------------------

Assume modest until proven otherwise.

- Feature theatrical/festival: hundreds to a few thousand dedicated viewers per strong screening run is a win; tens of thousands across a whole festival + small TV licence cycle would be a very good outcome for this class of film.
- European broadcaster licence: possible, not guaranteed; money modest if it happens (see paths already in this doc).
- Self-distribution + live screenings: room capacity math (50–200 seats, Robin + James set, ticket + merch). Repeatable if the night is good. This is how jazz economics actually work.
- YouTube long tail: Top 30 and shorts can out-reach the feature over years. Jazz YouTube is not MrBeast. Success is steady, not viral. A few strong episodes that keep getting recommended to Chet searchers is the realistic win.
- Streamers (Netflix et al.): not the plan. Near-zero probability. Do not spend mental energy or website copy on it.

Uncertainty note: none of these numbers are forecasts. They are order-of-magnitude sanity checks so we do not build strategy that only works if 500k strangers show up.

-----------------------------------------------------
9.5 Content products mapped to audiences
-----------------------------------------------------

| Product | Primary job | Who it serves |
|---|---|---|
| Feature (75–90 min) | Thesis + access; festival/broadcast/screening | A, B, gatekeepers, C (live nights) |
| Top 30 Albums series | Discovery engine; builds list and trust before/during film | A, D, C; feeds B later |
| Trip diary shorts | Human face of the production; channel fuel; not the film | C, D; some B |
| Interview anecdote clips (2–4 min) | Shareable proof of access; use carefully re: premiere | A, B; gatekeepers if exceptional |
| Live screening + set | Monetise Robin's real strength; build list | C first, then A/B in city |
| Trailer (~90 sec) | One object for festivals, site, social | Gatekeepers + everyone |
| Mailing list | The asset that survives all paths | Anyone who opts in |

Rule already in this strategy still stands: strongest stories stay out of promo until the film's window is decided. Top 30 and trip shorts run throughout; the film does not.

-----------------------------------------------------
9.6 Who is NOT the audience
-----------------------------------------------------

Be explicit so we stop accidentally writing for them:

- Not the general Netflix drama audience.
- Not "everyone who has heard My Funny Valentine once."
- Not lifestyle brand / cool aesthetics only (Weber already did cool; we are doing testimony and argument).
- Not people who want a full cradle-to-grave biopic. We are not making that film.
- Not advertisers who need mass CPM scale. If ads come later, they are narrow (jazz, Chet, European jazz cities, music doc keywords), not broad interest.

If a pitch, homepage sentence, or ad only makes sense for one of the above, cut it.

-----------------------------------------------------
9.7 Practical implications: website tone
-----------------------------------------------------

Public site should sound like a serious small film with a clear question, not like a studio campaign and not like a production wiki.

Tone targets:
- One logline above the fold (already good: European years waste vs real work).
- Proof of access (names, places, last-testimony urgency) without dumping the whole itinerary as marketing.
- Robin/repmusic as credible makers, not as a CV dump.
- Easy next step: watch something (when it exists), join list, follow channel. Until then: clear "in production" + contact.

Avoid:
- Pitching as if distribution is locked.
- Over-explaining Let's Get Lost (counterpart is useful; a full second film essay is not).
- Endless city cards and long credit prose for first-time visitors who only needed the question and the faces.

-----------------------------------------------------
9.8 Practical implications: Google Ads later
-----------------------------------------------------

Do not run ads until there is a concrete conversion (mailing list, trailer, ticket, or episode). When/if:

- Keywords: Chet Baker documentary, Chet Baker Europe, Chet Baker albums, jazz documentary, SteepleChase / late Chet adjacent terms, city+jazz only if screening in that city.
- Audiences: music doc interest, jazz, Chet Baker affinity if available; geo UK + NL/BE/IT/FR when relevant to screenings or language versions.
- Creative: one face (living collaborator) or one place (Pelzer / Amsterdam) + the question. Not "award-winning" language we have not earned.
- Budget: tiny tests. Kill anything that brings curiosity clicks with no list sign-up or watch-through.
- Never bid as if this is a mass entertainment product. CPC on junk terms will waste money and pull the wrong people.

-----------------------------------------------------
9.9 How we will learn (replace guesses with evidence)
-----------------------------------------------------

After Top 30 starts: which albums/episodes retain, which titles search, comments from real specialists vs drive-by.
After trip shorts: does Robin's existing audience share, or only strangers?
After first screening: who bought tickets (gig list, jazz society, random)?
After any festival submission: feedback is data even when the answer is no.

Rewrite 9.1–9.2 when we have that. Until then, act as if A + C are real and everything else is optional upside.

Status as of 21 Jul 2026 review of chasing-chet-website (single long page ~2300 words visible content, 5 nav items + extra sections).

Yes: the public site currently has too much content for a first visitor who only needs to answer "what is this, why should I care, what do I do next?"

What works and should stay near the top:
- Hero + logline (question is clear)
- Short "Europe half of the story" film section (tighten slightly)
- Voices grid (access is the asset; names matter)
- Contact / repmusic links

What to cut or demote for audience clarity:
1. Full "Let's Get Lost" essay + embedded trailer block
   Keep one short paragraph that positions Chasing Chet as the Europe counterpart. Move deep Weber context off the first scroll (or drop the embedded trailer). First visitors came for our film, not a second film's wiki page.
2. Six-city journey grid with stock-ish city photos
   Feels like a travel brochure. Replace with one sentence on the route + "in production, Europe Sept 2026," or a single map graphic later. City detail is for post-trip when we have our own stills and can prove we were there.
3. Long music/performance section and credits prose
   Fine for later; first visit needs less. Credits can be a short block: director, repmusic, in production.
4. Pull-quote section about Robin's live Chet performance
   Good social proof but it sits between thesis and counterpart and slows the page. Move below voices or into a "Director" subsection.
5. Dense legal/caption chrome and repeated internal links
   Necessary in footers; less necessary mid-page.

Suggested first-visit hierarchy:
1. Title + question + "in production 2026"
2. Three short paragraphs: gap (Europe), access (living collaborators), urgency (ages)
3. Voices (photos + roles)
4. One clear CTA (mailing list when ready; until then YouTube/repmusic + email)
5. Credits one-liner + contact

Everything else (deep counterpart, city tour, full music essay) is second-scroll or post-trip enrichment.
